Hiking Tahoma

About Mount Rainier

Mount Rainier is my favorite place to hike. It's a 14,411 foot volcano featuring an astonishing diversity of terrain. Lush forests, wildflower meadows, alpine tundra, snowfields, glaciers, bizarre volcanic rock formations, wild woolly rivers, and a plethora of waterfalls can all be experienced within a single hike. Even when you are 'just' hiking through trees, constant changes in altitude plus the rain shadow difference between east and west sides of the mountain (east is drier, west very wet) mean no two Rainier forests are the same.

And then there are the animals. I've seen deer, elk, marmots aplenty, grouse, foxes, rabbits, and bear. Still waiting for a cougar encounter though! Before you panic about those last two: Rainier animals are used to hikers, and negative encounters vanishingly rare. Treat them with respect, avoid surprising them, keep your food out of their way, and you won't have any trouble.

Hiking trails vary from popular (aka massively crowded) and easy to access, to obscure and remote. Trails are generally well maintained and signposted, so navigation is not difficult. A major exception is if you're travelling over snowfields, such as to Camp Muir, over Panhandle Gap or Spray Park before they melt out, or hiking early/late in the season. Do not hike over snow unless you are prepared to navigate through a white-out, or to hunker down and wait if the weather turns on you! It's a true saying that the mountain makes its own weather, and people have died from underestimating what that means at high altitude.

Another potential risk (which mostly affects backpackers) is the more remote river crossings, where bridges are prone to washing away and glacial meltwater can be challenging to ford if you want to cross before the park service gets around to replacing them.

Rainier is at its best between July and September. There can also be great hiking in the rump seasons of May, June, October, and November, but these months require more self sufficiency and attention to weather forecasts. The road up to Paradise is kept ploughed during the winter, so high altitude snow sports remain accessible all year.

This place is crammed with history. Starting with the name of the mountain itself: there is a fascinating debate over whether it should be Rainier or Tahoma. Within the park there are beautiful place names (Mowich Lake, Ohanapecosh River, Wauhaukaupauken Falls) and also some that make me cringe (Indian Bar, Indian Henry's Hunting Grounds, and the especially inappropriate Squaw Lake). I like to take a moment at these beautiful places to think about the not-so-beautiful history that came before me, and acknowledge the people whose land this once was.

Access

There are four entrances to the park: Nisqually (which leads to Longmire and Paradise) at the southwest, a northeast entrance leading to White River and Sunrise, Carbon River (leading to Mowich Lake) at the northwest, and the southeast Stevens Canyon entrance.

A fee is required to enter the National Park. If you plan on visiting more than once, it's much cheaper to buy an annual pass. Or if you only plan on visiting once, WHAT IS WRONG WITH YOU??? :-)

Parking spaces tend to fill up early, and waiting lines at the park entrances can be long on sunny summer weekends. I highly recommend arriving early to skip the crowds, find solitude on the trail, and boost your chances of seeing wildlife. Some of my most treasured memories are from hikes where I arrived at Sunrise as early as 4 AM, then watched the sun come up around me while already on the trail.

Backpacking

The epic Wonderland Trail (90+ miles circumnavigating the mountain) is of course the jewel in Rainier's backpacking crown, but there are also many options for shorter loops or section hikes. Backpacking on Rainier can feel remote (few other people, a long way from road access) but also strangely tame as there are designated campsites with maintained pit toilets and bear poles for hanging your food.

Permits are required to camp on Rainier (because with designated campsites, there are only a fixed number of spaces to go around). The normal system is that 2/3 of available capacity is allocated through a lottery during March, while 1/3 is held in reserve for walkup permits, which are issued on a first-come-first-served basis to anyone who arrives in person at a ranger station for a trip starting that day or the day after. Some years are different, though. In 2016, all permits were walkup due to a software problem. In 2020, all permits were issued in advance due to the COVID pandemic.

Resources

Mount Rainier Recreational Forecast - the most useful weather info I have found so far.

Trails and Backcountry Camp Conditions - latest info from the park service about closures, road status, etc.

Rainier webcams - check weather conditions and snowpack level for yourself, in realtime.

Washington Trails Association trip reports - read about hikes others have recently completed.

Wonderland Guides - great info about Wonderland camp selection, permit process, food caching etc.

Wonderland Trail Facebook group - members-only group, very helpful for any Rainier backpacking questions (not just the full Wonderland).
